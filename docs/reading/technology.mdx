## Passage 1: Artificial Intelligence in Daily Life

**Artificial intelligence** (AI) is no longer a concept from science fiction — it has become a part of our everyday lives. From **voice assistants** like Siri and Alexa to **recommendation** systems on streaming platforms, AI technology is everywhere.

One of the most **significant** applications of AI is in healthcare. AI systems can analyze medical images and help doctors **diagnose** diseases more accurately and quickly. In education, AI-powered tools can **personalize** learning experiences, **adapting** to each student's pace and style.

However, the rapid development of AI also raises **concerns**. Many people worry about job **displacement** — the possibility that machines will replace human workers. Others are concerned about **privacy**, as AI systems often require large amounts of personal data to function effectively. Finding the right **balance** between **innovation** and **regulation** will be one of the great challenges of our time.

## Passage 2: The Rise of Electric Vehicles

Electric vehicles (EVs) are **transforming** the automobile industry. With growing **awareness** of climate change, more and more consumers are choosing EVs over traditional gasoline-powered cars.

The **advantages** of electric vehicles are clear. They produce zero direct **emissions**, which helps reduce air pollution. They are also cheaper to **maintain**, as electric motors have fewer moving parts than internal combustion engines. Additionally, the cost of **batteries** has dropped **dramatically** over the past decade, making EVs more **affordable**.

Despite these benefits, challenges remain. The most common concern is "range **anxiety**" — the fear that the car will run out of power before reaching a charging station. To address this, governments and companies are **investing** heavily in building more charging **infrastructure**. Experts predict that by 2030, EVs will **account** for a significant **proportion** of all new car sales worldwide.

## Passage 3: Social Media and Mental Health

Social media platforms have **revolutionized** the way we communicate and share information. However, a growing body of research suggests that **excessive** use of social media can have **negative** effects on mental health.

Studies have found that spending too much time on social media can lead to feelings of **loneliness**, **anxiety**, and low self-esteem. The constant **comparison** with others' seemingly perfect lives can make people feel **inadequate**. Furthermore, the **addictive** nature of social media — designed to keep users **scrolling** — can **interfere** with sleep, study, and face-to-face relationships.

That said, social media is not **inherently** harmful. When used **moderately**, it can help people stay connected, find **communities** of shared interests, and access valuable information. The key is to be **mindful** of how much time you spend online and to **cultivate** healthy digital habits.

## Passage 4: Space Exploration in the 21st Century

Space exploration has entered a new era. Unlike the government-led space programs of the 20th century, today's space industry is **increasingly** driven by **private** companies such as SpaceX, Blue Origin, and Virgin Galactic.

These companies have **dramatically** reduced the cost of launching rockets through **reusable** rocket technology. SpaceX's Falcon 9, for example, can land and be **reflown** multiple times, cutting launch costs by up to 80%. This has opened the door to **ambitious** projects such as building a permanent human **settlement** on Mars.

Space exploration also offers **practical** benefits for life on Earth. **Satellite** technology powers GPS navigation, weather **forecasting**, and global communications. The research **conducted** in space has led to advances in medicine, materials science, and environmental **monitoring**. As technology continues to **evolve**, the **boundaries** of human exploration will keep expanding.

## Passage 5: The Internet of Things

The **Internet of Things** (IoT) refers to the network of physical devices — from home **appliances** to industrial machines — that are connected to the internet and can **communicate** with each other.

In smart homes, IoT devices allow homeowners to control lighting, heating, and security systems **remotely** through their smartphones. Smart **refrigerators** can track food supplies and even suggest recipes. Wearable devices like fitness trackers **monitor** heart rate, sleep patterns, and physical activity, helping users make **informed** health decisions.

In industry, IoT technology is used to **optimize** manufacturing processes, **predict** equipment failures before they happen, and manage supply chains more **efficiently**. However, the **widespread** adoption of IoT also raises **security** concerns. With billions of devices connected to the internet, each one represents a **potential** entry point for hackers. Protecting these devices from **cyber** attacks is a **critical** challenge for the technology industry.
