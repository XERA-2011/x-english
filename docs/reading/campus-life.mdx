## Passage 1: Choosing a Major

Choosing a college major is one of the most important **decisions** a student has to make. Some students know exactly what they want to study from the very beginning, while others feel **uncertain** and may change their major several times.

Experts **recommend** that students explore different subjects during their first year. Taking a **variety** of courses allows students to discover their true interests and strengths. It's also helpful to talk to **professors**, upper-class students, and career **counselors** for advice.

Students shouldn't choose a major based **solely** on future salary. While earning potential is important, studying something you're **passionate** about will make the college experience more **enjoyable** and **rewarding**. A student who loves what they study is more likely to succeed in the long run.

## Passage 2: Life in a College Dormitory

Living in a college dormitory is a **unique** experience that brings both **challenges** and **opportunities**. For many students, it is their first time living away from home, which means they must learn to be **independent**.

One of the biggest challenges is sharing a room with one or more **roommates**. Different people have different habits — some prefer to study late at night, while others like to wake up early. **Communication** is key to **resolving** these differences. Setting clear rules about noise, cleanliness, and **visitors** can help prevent conflicts.

On the positive side, dormitory life provides a great **opportunity** to build **friendships** and develop social skills. Students learn to **compromise**, respect others' space, and work as a team. These skills are **invaluable** not only in college but also in future careers and relationships.

## Passage 3: The Value of Extracurricular Activities

Participating in **extracurricular** activities is an important part of the college experience. These activities, which range from sports teams to debate clubs to volunteer organizations, offer **numerous** benefits beyond the classroom.

First, extracurricular activities help students develop **leadership** and **teamwork** skills. Students who **organize** events, manage budgets, or lead meetings gain practical experience that cannot be learned from textbooks. Second, these activities provide an opportunity to meet people with similar interests, expanding students' social **networks**.

**Admittedly**, balancing schoolwork and extracurricular activities can be difficult. Students must learn to manage their time **wisely** and avoid **overcommitting** themselves. The key is to **prioritize** quality over quantity — it's better to be deeply involved in one or two activities than to **superficially** participate in many.

## Passage 4: Preparing for Final Exams

Final exam season is one of the most **stressful** periods for college students. However, with proper **preparation** and smart study habits, it doesn't have to be **overwhelming**.

Start studying early. Instead of **cramming** everything into one night, **spread** your study sessions over several weeks. Research shows that **spaced** learning — reviewing material at **regular** intervals — is much more effective than marathon study sessions.

Form a study group with classmates. Explaining **concepts** to others helps **reinforce** your own understanding. You can also **benefit** from hearing different **perspectives** and filling in gaps in your knowledge.

Don't **neglect** your physical health during exam season. Getting enough sleep, eating well, and taking regular breaks are all **essential** for maintaining peak mental **performance**. Remember: a well-rested brain learns more **efficiently** than a tired one.

## Passage 5: Part-Time Jobs for Students

Many college students take on **part-time** jobs during their studies. While the extra income is helpful, the benefits **extend** far beyond money.

Working part-time teaches students **valuable** life skills such as time management, responsibility, and interpersonal **communication**. Students learn how to deal with customers, follow **instructions**, and meet **deadlines** — skills that will serve them well in their future careers.

However, students need to be **cautious** about working too many hours. Studies show that students who work more than 20 hours per week tend to have lower **academic** performance. The ideal balance is to work 10-15 hours per week, which provides financial support without **sacrificing** study time.
